Full Funnel Audit — What We Found

Page-by-page findings

Live crawl of milligram.com conducted April 2026. Each page audited against conversion best practices and friction signals.

🏠 Homepage

Aspirational, not action-led

Hero headline "What can you make on Paper?" is brand-led but doesn't communicate a benefit or offer. Generic "SHOP NOW" CTA gives no scent.
Zero social proof above the fold — no review count, no customer number, no star rating aggregate anywhere on the homepage.
Announcement bar rotating: Buy 2 Get 1 FREE on notebooks + $69 free shipping threshold — good promotional visibility.
"Our Favourites" product carousel shows no star ratings on cards, leaving browsing customers with no confidence signals.
📋 PLP — Notebooks

Large banner steals viewport

Category header takes up nearly the full first viewport on desktop — first product doesn't appear until significant scroll, increasing drop-off risk.
256 products listed with no star ratings on any product card. Brand, name, price and "+ Add to Bag" visible — social proof completely absent.
6 filter categories (Sort By, Brand, Colour, Size, Layout, Price) all collapsed by default. Heavy filter overhead with no active filter count.
Quick-add "+ Add to Bag" button present on PLP cards — reduces friction for decisive buyers without requiring PDP visit.
🛍 PDP

Trust signals missing at decision point

No star rating or review count visible anywhere in the ATC zone. Reviews widget exists but shows "No reviews collected for this product yet."
Delivery promise ("Ready to ship · Click & Collect") appears below the ATC button — requires scrolling to find, which most users won't do.
"Customers Also Bought" cross-sell exists but is buried 3 scrolls below the fold, after a brand storytelling block and empty reviews section.
Afterpay instalment info displayed near price. Full-width prominent ATC button — good visual hierarchy for primary action.
🛒 Cart Drawer

Cross-sell exists — but has no add button

Cart cross-sell "You May Also Like" shows 2+ products with name and price but zero quick-add buttons. Users must navigate away to a PDP to add any item.
Free shipping progress bar visible ($49.01 gap on a $19.99 cart) but cross-sell items aren't contextually tied to closing that gap.
"Friends For Life" loyalty block sits between cross-sell and checkout CTA — potentially distracting from conversion at highest-intent moment.
Cart drawer opens on ATC click (no page redirect). Free shipping progress bar, qty adjuster, and CHECKOUT CTA all present.

Key Observations

What the crawl is telling us

Three compounding issues — each individually meaningful, together they explain meaningful AOV and CVR drag.

0

Quick-add buttons in the cart cross-sell

Milligram has a "You May Also Like" section inside their cart drawer — but clicking a recommended product navigates away from the cart entirely to the PDP. There is no mechanism to add a second item without leaving the checkout flow. This is a direct friction point at their highest-intent page.

$49

Average gap to free shipping on a single-notebook order

The $69 AUD free shipping threshold creates a natural AOV lever. A customer buying one $19.99 notebook is $49.01 away from free shipping. The cart already shows a progress bar, but the cross-sell items aren't framed around closing that gap — missing the most powerful nudge available.

3

Scrolls below the fold before you see a cross-sell on PDP

The PDP's "Customers Also Bought" section appears after: the product images, the delivery block, the product description, the specifications, a full-width brand storytelling banner, and an empty reviews section. Fewer than 20% of mobile users reach this depth. The cross-sell is there — it just needs to be moved up.

The Problem
The cart drawer's "You May Also Like" cross-sell shows product images, names, and prices — but has no add-to-cart button. A customer who wants to add the recommended Smiley Sticky Note Pad ($9.99) must click through to the PDP, then add to cart from there — losing their cart context and increasing abandonment risk at the highest-intent moment in the funnel.
Hypothesis
If we add a quick-add "+" button to each cart cross-sell tile AND reframe the cross-sell headline to reference the free shipping gap ("You're $X from free shipping — pair your order with:"), then AOV will increase because we remove the friction from adding secondary items and create goal-gradient urgency around the $69 free shipping threshold.
What changes
Control: Cross-sell tiles with image, name, price — no add button. Headline: "You May Also Like."

Variant: Cross-sell tiles gain a "+" quick-add button that fires an AJAX add-to-cart (no page navigation). Headline dynamically reads: "You're $[X] from free shipping — these pair well." Cross-sell product mix expanded to include complementary cross-category items (pens + notebook, pen holder + notebook) rather than same-brand only.
ICE Rationale
Impact (8): Cart is the highest-intent touchpoint. Even small friction removal at this stage has outsized revenue impact. The $69 threshold creates a natural goal gradient — customers are primed to respond to nudges that help them close a visible gap.

Confidence (8): Cart cross-sell quick-add is one of the most reliably positive interventions in e-commerce CRO. The mechanism (AJAX ATC) is proven at scale across thousands of Shopify stores. Free shipping bar + contextual copy is validated by numerous published case studies.

Ease (7): Shopify Liquid template edit to cart drawer — add button renders using existing cart.js add endpoint. Dynamic headline requires one Liquid variable. No third-party apps required.

The Problem
Milligram's PDP ATC zone — the area where purchase decisions are made — contains only a price, an Afterpay instalment note, and an ADD TO CART button. The delivery promise ("Ready to ship · Click & Collect") is present but hidden below the ATC button, requiring a scroll that most users never make. There are no reviews, no trust badges, and no explicit reassurance about returns or checkout security visible at the moment of decision.
Hypothesis
If we relocate the "Ready to ship" delivery promise ABOVE the ATC button and add a three-element trust bar (delivery / returns / secure checkout), then add-to-cart rate will increase because we address purchase hesitation at the exact moment it occurs — the gap between "I want this" and "I'll click Add to Cart."
What changes
Control: Price → Afterpay → ATC button → [scroll required] → Delivery / Click & Collect tabs.

Variant: Price → Afterpay → 3-icon trust bar ("✓ Ready to ship · ✓ Free returns · ✓ Secure checkout") → ATC button → Delivery tabs (remaining). Optionally: a micro loyalty nudge below ATC ("Earn points with Friends For Life — join free").
ICE Rationale
Impact (7): PDP to ATC is the most important conversion step on a product-led store. Even a 3–5% ATC lift compounds significantly across all sessions. The current gap is wide — Afterpay is shown but shipping and returns information is not.

Confidence (8): Delivery promise relocation is a well-documented CRO lever. Baymard Institute research consistently shows that shipping cost/time uncertainty is a top-3 reason for checkout abandonment. Moving the signal to before the ATC click pre-empts the anxiety rather than reacting to it.

Ease (7): Shopify product template edit — rearrange existing Liquid blocks and add a static trust bar component. No new apps required. Can be built in a day.

The Problem
Milligram's PLP product cards show brand, product name, price, and a quick-add button — but no star ratings. In a 256-product catalogue, customers have no way to sort by quality signals or identify popular products from the browse view. The reviews infrastructure exists (Shopify Reviews app installed, widget present on PDPs) but it isn't surfaced at the PLP level, meaning social proof stops at the PDP — not at the discovery stage where it matters most for click-through.
Hypothesis
If we surface star ratings and review counts on PLP product cards for products that have reviews, then PDP click-through rate and add-to-cart rate will increase because customers can identify high-confidence products at the browse stage, reducing hesitation and improving the quality of intent arriving at the PDP.
Prerequisite
Several audited PDPs showed "No reviews collected for this product yet." Before this test can run at meaningful scale, a post-purchase review request sequence should be activated (Shopify Email or Judge.me). Reviews collection is likely an ops gap, not a technical one — the widget already renders on the PDP.
ICE Rationale
Impact (7): PLP star ratings have shown +10–18% PDP CTR lifts in comparable specialty retail audits. For a 256-product catalogue with heavy discovery browsing, confidence signals at the card level meaningfully reduce choice paralysis.

Confidence (7): Established best practice for multi-product catalogues. Confidence is slightly tempered by the prerequisite — this test is less impactful until reviews exist on the products being surfaced.

Ease (6): The Liquid template edit is straightforward. The constraint is seeding sufficient reviews first. Recommend launching the review collection sequence in parallel so that by the time this test is ready, rated products can be surfaced.

Why We Expect These to Work

The evidence behind each hypothesis

🛒

Cart friction is the most expensive problem in e-commerce

Baymard Institute puts average cart abandonment at 70.19%. The top reasons are unexpected costs and complexity — both of which Milligram's cart partially addresses (free shipping bar is present), but then re-introduces through a cross-sell with no add mechanism. Removing that navigation step is a direct abandonment mitigation.

🎯

Goal-gradient psychology drives spend toward thresholds

The closer a customer is to a reward (free shipping), the faster they move toward it. Milligram already shows the progress bar — but the cross-sell isn't framed in terms of that progress. Saying "You're $49 away — here's what to add" converts the cross-sell from passive discovery to active goal completion.

⭐

Social proof reduces cognitive load at the decision moment

When a customer arrives at a PDP with zero visible social proof, they bear the full cognitive burden of evaluating the product alone. A trust bar with delivery, returns, and security signals reduces that load by confirming the conditions of purchase — allowing the customer to focus on wanting the product, not worrying about what happens if they don't.

📋

Star ratings at browse stage filter intent, not just inform

A customer clicking through to a PDP having already seen 4.8★ (124 reviews) arrives in a fundamentally different buying state than one who has seen no signal at all. PLP ratings reduce the number of "disappointment visits" (click → decide not to buy) and improve the quality of intent arriving at add-to-cart.

📦

Delivery certainty is a pre-purchase anxiety trigger

For a brand with physical stores, "Ready to ship" and "Click & Collect" are powerful reassurances — but only if visible before the purchase commitment. Currently these signals require scrolling past the ATC button. Moving them above the button means every customer sees them before deciding, not after.

🔁

Reviews infrastructure is already installed — it just isn't active

The reviews widget renders on every PDP. The gap isn't technical — it's operational. Milligram needs a post-purchase review request sequence. Once seeded, the same tests 2 and 3 become significantly more powerful as product-level social proof replaces the current "no reviews" placeholder.

Risks & Mitigations

What could go wrong

Risk
Why it matters
Mitigation
Cart clutter
Test 1
Adding quick-add buttons to the cart cross-sell increases visual complexity. If the drawer feels crowded, it could slow checkout intent rather than accelerate it.
Keep the cross-sell to 2 products max. Use a compact tile design. Monitor checkout CTA click rate as a secondary metric — if it drops, the variant loses regardless of AOV.
Irrelevant cross-sells
Test 1
Current cart cross-sells show only same-brand, same-category products (notebooks alongside notebooks). A pen recommended to someone buying a notebook is more compelling than another notebook.
Curate cross-sell product list manually for the test. Pair pens + notebooks, pen holders + notebooks, sticky pads + notebooks. Confirm with Milligram which cross-category pairings perform in their physical stores.
Trust bar looks templated
Test 2
Generic "Secure checkout / Free returns / Fast shipping" bars can read as boilerplate and be tuned out by experienced shoppers if they don't feel brand-specific.
Use Milligram's specific language: "Ready to ship today" (not "Fast shipping"), "Easy returns" (not generic). Match existing brand tone. Consider small Milligram-branded icons rather than generic symbols.
Insufficient reviews
Test 3
If most products show 0–2 reviews, surfacing star ratings on PLP could backfire — a product with 1 review at 3★ shown prominently on browse may suppress CTR rather than lift it.
Only render star ratings on cards where review count ≥ 5. Hide the rating element on low-review products. Launch review collection sequence 60 days before this test goes live.
Mobile UX degradation
All Tests
All three changes add elements to existing layouts. On smaller viewports, additional trust rows and cross-sell tiles compete for limited screen space.
QA all variants on iPhone SE (smallest common viewport) and Samsung Galaxy A-series before launch. Design mobile-first for tests 1 and 2. GrowthBook segment results by device type.

Next Steps

From approved to live

1

Client scoping call — confirm baseline data access

Request GA4 property access, Shopify collaborator account (read-only), and gross margin % or Shopify cost-per-item data. Confirm active promotions calendar and any no-go zones (e.g. checkout page, payment configuration). Ask about in-store purchase data — Milligram's physical store co-purchase patterns are likely the best signal for cross-sell product selection.

Stage 2 Kickoff
2

GA4 + Shopify order data analysis

Run co-purchase frequency analysis on last 90 days of Shopify orders to identify the highest-frequency product pairings for Test 1's cross-sell curation. Pull GA4 PDP → ATC funnel per product to confirm Test 2 opportunity size. Confirm PLP drop-off data (exit rate by scroll depth) to size Test 3 impact.

Data Phase
3

Launch post-purchase review request sequence (parallel track)

Activate Shopify's built-in review request email or configure Judge.me (if preferred). Target: 60-day window to collect ≥ 5 reviews on top-50 products before Test 3 launches. This is an operational change, not an A/B test — it should start immediately regardless of test timeline.

Parallel Track · No Test Dependency
4

GrowthBook setup — feature flags + metrics

Create feature flags: cart-crosssell-quick-add, pdp-atc-trust-stack, plp-star-ratings. Configure GA4 event-based metrics for ATC rate, cross-sell add rate, and AOV. Set 95% confidence threshold. Tests 1 and 2 can run simultaneously; Test 3 runs after review collection is sufficient.

Experimentation Setup
5

Build & QA — Tests 1 and 2 first

Shopify Liquid edits to cart drawer template (cart-crosssell-quick-add feature flag) and product template ATC zone (pdp-atc-trust-stack). QA across: Chrome/Safari/Firefox, iOS Safari, Android Chrome, viewport widths 375px → 1440px. Confirm AJAX add-to-cart fires correctly and cart count updates without page reload.

Dev Sprint
6

Launch Tests 1 & 2 → read at 14 days, declare at 21 days

Monitor daily for the first 5 days for any anomalies (cart abandonment spike, ATC rate crash). Read preliminary results at 14 days — if strong positive signal, begin shipping variant to 100%. Declare statistical significance at 21 days minimum. Document winning variants for rollout.
