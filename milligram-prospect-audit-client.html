What We Found — Full Funnel Audit

Page-by-page findings

We audited your homepage, product listing pages, product detail pages, and cart drawer. Here's what the funnel looks like right now.

🏠 Homepage

Brand presence strong, conversion signals missing

Beautiful editorial hero — but no benefit-led messaging or offer visible above the fold. "SHOP NOW" CTA gives no scent about what to shop for.
No social proof above the fold — no review count, no customer number, no star aggregates anywhere on the homepage.
Announcement bar rotating the BOGO notebook offer and $69 free shipping threshold — good promotional visibility throughout the session.
"Our Favourites" product carousel displays with no star ratings — a customer browsing can't identify your most-loved products at a glance.
📋 Product Listing

256 products, zero social proof signals

Category banner takes up the full first viewport — products don't appear until significant scroll, increasing browse drop-off before customers can engage.
Product cards show brand, name, price, and add button — but no star ratings on any of 256 products. Customers have no confidence signal to filter by.
Six filter categories all collapsed by default. For a 256-product catalogue, customers need help narrowing quickly — collapsed filters add friction.
Quick-add "Add to Bag" button on PLP cards — good friction reduction for decisive buyers who already know what they want.
🛍 Product Page

Add-to-cart moment lacks reassurance

No reviews visible anywhere in the add-to-cart zone. The reviews widget is installed, but most products show "No reviews collected yet."
Delivery promise ("Ready to ship · Click & Collect") is positioned below the Add to Cart button — customers don't see it before they decide whether to buy.
"Customers Also Bought" section exists but is 3+ scrolls below the fold, after a brand block and an empty reviews section — very few customers reach it.
Afterpay shown near price — smart for reducing perceived price barrier. Prominent full-width Add to Cart button with clear visual hierarchy.
🛒 Cart Drawer

Cross-sell in place — but no way to add

"You May Also Like" cross-sell shows products with images and prices — but has no add-to-cart button. Customers must navigate away to a product page to add any recommended item.
Free shipping progress bar is live ($49.01 gap on a $19.99 cart) but the recommended products aren't framed around closing that gap — missing the most compelling nudge.
Cart drawer opens on add-to-cart click (no full page redirect) — modern UX that keeps context. Clear CHECKOUT button with total displayed.
Friends For Life loyalty program referenced in cart — good for account creation, though placement between cross-sell and checkout could be refined.

The Headline Numbers

Three specific gaps driving revenue loss

Each of these is measurable, testable, and fixable without a redesign.

0

Quick-add buttons in your cart cross-sell

Your cart drawer already recommends products — but there's no button to add them. Every customer who wants to add a second item has to navigate away from the cart, find the product, and add it from the PDP. Most don't come back. A single "+ Add" button on each recommended product removes that friction entirely.

$49

Average gap to free shipping on a single-notebook order

Your free shipping threshold is $69. A customer buying one notebook at $19.99 is $49 away. The progress bar tells them the gap — but nothing tells them what to add to close it. Reframing the cross-sell around "here's what gets you to free shipping" turns passive discovery into active goal completion.

3+

Scrolls required to reach a cross-sell on your product pages

Your "Customers Also Bought" section sits below the product description, specifications, a full-width brand block, and an empty reviews section. On mobile, fewer than 20% of users scroll that far. Moving the cross-sell above the fold — or relocating it adjacent to the ATC button — would expose it to the majority of your traffic.

What the problem is
Your cart drawer already recommends products in a "You May Also Like" carousel — but there's no button to add them to the cart. A customer who sees the Smiley Sticky Note Pad recommended and wants to add it has to click through to its PDP and add from there. This navigation away from the cart increases the chance they don't return to complete the purchase.
What we'll test
Control (current): Cart cross-sell tiles show image, name, price. No add button. Headline: "You May Also Like."

Variant: Each cross-sell tile gets a quick-add "+" button that adds the item to cart via AJAX (no page navigation). Headline dynamically shows: "You're $49 from free shipping — these pair well." Product selection also expands to include cross-category complements (pens paired with notebooks, pen holders, etc.).
Why we expect it to work
Goal-gradient psychology: the closer a customer is to a reward (free shipping), the more motivated they become to close the gap. Your progress bar already creates the goal — we just need to connect it to the right products with a clear action. Removing the navigation step from "see recommendation" to "add to cart" directly reduces the drop-off between these two states.

What the problem is
Your product pages show price, Afterpay, and an Add to Cart button — then nothing else until the customer scrolls. The delivery information ("Ready to ship · Click & Collect") exists, but it's positioned below the ATC button, which means most customers never see it before they make their decision. What we'll test
Control (current): Price → Afterpay → ATC button → [scroll] → Delivery info.

Variant: Price → Afterpay → a compact 3-element trust bar ("✓ Ready to ship today · ✓ Free returns · ✓ Secure checkout") → ATC button. Optionally, a loyalty micro-nudge below the button ("Earn points with Friends For Life — join free") to promote account creation at the highest-intent moment.
Why we expect it to work
Research from the Baymard Institute consistently identifies shipping cost and delivery uncertainty as top-3 reasons for checkout abandonment. Customers experience purchase anxiety before clicking Add to Cart — not just at checkout. Moving the delivery reassurance to before the click addresses the hesitation before it becomes an exit.

5
The Evidence Behind Each Test

Why we expect these to move the needle

🛒

Cart friction is the most expensive problem in e-commerce

Average cart abandonment sits at 70%. The most common cause is friction between "I want something" and "I added it." Your cross-sell has the right intent — it just needs an add button to execute on it.

🎯

Free shipping thresholds create natural AOV momentum

Showing customers they're close to a goal (free shipping) and giving them easy options to close that gap is one of the highest-ROI cart interventions available. No discount needed — the "reward" is already in your site architecture.

📦

Delivery certainty reduces add-to-cart hesitation

Baymard Institute research shows shipping uncertainty is a top-3 reason customers don't complete a purchase. "Ready to ship" is a powerful reassurance — but only if the customer sees it before they decide, not after scrolling past the Add to Cart button.

⭐

Social proof at the browse stage filters intent, not just informs

A customer who clicks through to a PDP having already seen 4.8★ from 124 reviews arrives in a fundamentally different buying state. Star ratings on PLP cards improve the quality of intent reaching your product pages — and the add-to-cart rate that follows.

🔁

Your reviews infrastructure is already installed

The reviews widget renders on every product page — it's just showing "No reviews yet" because the post-purchase request sequence hasn't been activated. This is a quick operational fix that unlocks both Test 2 and Test 3 power.

📊

All tests are controlled — no risk of launching a losing change site-wide

Every test runs as a GrowthBook A/B experiment with a 50/50 traffic split. If a variant doesn't win at 95% confidence, it doesn't ship. You only deploy what the data confirms works for your customers specifically.
